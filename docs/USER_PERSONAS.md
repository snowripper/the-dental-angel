# The Dental Angel — User Personas for UI Design

These personas are based on research into dental patient behavior, health literacy studies, mobile usability for older adults, and dental anxiety data. They're designed to guide every UI decision — from button sizes to word choices to screen layouts.

---

## Persona 1: Susan — "The Anxious Planner"

**She is your #1 user. Design the entire app for her first.**

### Who She Is
- **Age:** 58
- **Location:** Sacramento, CA
- **Occupation:** Retired school administrator
- **Family:** Married, two adult children, helps with grandkids
- **Phone:** iPhone 13, bought it because her daughter set it up for her

### What Happened
Susan went to the dentist for a cleaning. The dentist said she needs a crown on tooth #14, and mentioned the tooth next to it might need a root canal "down the road." The treatment plan says $1,800 for the crown. She left the office feeling blindsided. She wasn't expecting anything beyond a cleaning.

### Her Emotional State (When She Opens the App)
- **Anxiety level: High.** She's not afraid of the dentist, but she's afraid of being taken advantage of. Her friend told her a story about getting unnecessary work done.
- **Confusion level: High.** She doesn't understand the difference between a crown, a cap, an onlay, or a veneer. The treatment plan uses codes like "D2740" that mean nothing to her.
- **Financial stress: Medium-High.** She's on a fixed retirement income. $1,800 is real money. She wants to know: "Is this price fair? Is this even necessary?"
- **Trust level: Cautious.** She trusts her dentist... mostly. But she's heard stories. She wants someone neutral to help her think it through.
- **Time pressure: Moderate.** The office wants to schedule the crown for next week. She feels rushed.

### How She Uses Her Phone
- **Comfort level: Low-Medium.** She can text, use Facebook, take photos, and use Google Maps. That's about it.
- **What trips her up:**
  - Tiny text she has to squint at
  - Buttons that are too close together (she hits the wrong one)
  - Being asked to "sign up" or "create an account" before she can see if the app is even useful
  - Unexpected pop-ups that she doesn't know how to dismiss
  - Screens with too many options — she freezes and doesn't know where to start
  - Accidentally swiping to a different screen and not knowing how to get back
- **What she's comfortable with:**
  - Scrolling up and down
  - Tapping one big obvious button
  - Typing a message (slowly, with one finger, but she'll do it)
  - Taking a photo if you tell her to point and tap
- **Her glasses:** She wears reading glasses. Sometimes she's using the app without them (in bed, on the couch). Text needs to be BIG.
- **Her fingers:** She sometimes taps imprecisely. Buttons need to be generously sized with plenty of space between them.

### What She Wants from The Dental Angel
1. **"Is this normal?"** — She wants reassurance that the recommended treatment is (or isn't) something most dentists would suggest
2. **"Is the price fair?"** — She wants to know if $1,800 is in the right ballpark for her area
3. **"What should I ask my dentist?"** — She wants to go back to her appointment feeling prepared, not helpless
4. **"Explain it like I'm not a dentist"** — She wants plain English, not dental jargon

### What Would Make Her Leave the App
- Having to create an account before she can try it
- A wall of text with no clear starting point
- Being asked questions she can't answer ("What's the CDT code?")
- Feeling like the app is trying to sell her something before helping her
- Seeing the word "disclaimer" in a scary legal block before she even starts
- The app crashing or freezing
- Not understanding what to do next on any screen

### What Would Make Her Tell Her Friends About It
- Feeling heard — like the app actually understood her situation
- Getting a clear, simple explanation she can understand
- Walking into her next appointment feeling confident
- The "questions to ask" list — she can literally hand her phone to the dentist
- Feeling like someone is on her side (without bashing her dentist)

### UI Design Rules for Susan

| Principle | Specific Guideline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Text size** | 16px minimum everywhere. 18-20px for anything she needs to read carefully (chat messages, treatment explanations, scores). Never go below 16px, even for captions. |
| **Touch targets** | 48px minimum height for all tappable elements. 12px minimum spacing between tappable items. |
| **Contrast** | Body text should be dark (#292524) on light backgrounds. Never use light gray text on white. |
| **One action per screen** | Every screen should have ONE obvious thing to do. If Susan has to think about where to tap, we've failed. |
| **Progress indicators** | When something is loading or processing, show a clear spinner or message. She'll think it's broken otherwise. |
| **Error recovery** | If she taps the wrong thing, make it effortless to go back. Big, obvious back buttons. Never punish mistakes. |
| **No jargon anywhere** | Not in buttons, not in labels, not in settings. "Your treatment" not "Treatment plan analysis." |
| **Calm, not clinical** | Warm colors, rounded corners, friendly language. This should feel like talking to a kind friend, not visiting a medical website. |

## Persona 2: Maria — "The Worried Mom"

### Who She Is
- **Age:** 42
- **Location:** Houston, TX
- **Occupation:** Office manager at a plumbing company
- **Family:** Single mom, 14-year-old daughter, 10-year-old son
- **Phone:** Samsung Galaxy A54, uses it constantly for work and personal

### What Happened
Maria's son needs braces. The orthodontist quoted $5,200 for traditional braces or $6,800 for Invisalign. Her dental insurance covers $1,500. She's looking at $3,700-$5,300 out of pocket. She also needs to figure out if traditional braces or Invisalign is better for a 10-year-old — the orthodontist seemed to push Invisalign but she wonders if that's because it costs more.

### Her Emotional State
- **Anxiety level: Medium.** She's not anxious about the dental work itself — it's her kid, she just wants what's best. But the money stresses her out.
- **Guilt factor: High.** She feels guilty that she might choose the cheaper option. She wonders: "Am I being a bad mom if I pick traditional braces to save $2,000?"
- **Financial stress: High.** She's already stretching her budget. She's wondering if she can do a payment plan, and whether the orthodontist's in-house financing is a good deal.
- **Trust level: Skeptical.** She read online that orthodontists make more money on Invisalign. She wants an unbiased perspective.
- **Time pressure: Low.** Braces aren't urgent. She has time to research.

### How She Uses Her Phone
- **Comfort level: High.** She's on her phone all day — texting clients, checking email, scrolling social media, shopping online. She downloads apps without hesitation.
- **What trips her up:**
  - Apps that waste her time with long onboarding flows
  - Being asked to pay before she sees value
  - Cluttered screens where she can't find what she needs quickly
  - Apps that don't work well on her Android phone
- **Her expectations:** She's used to polished apps (Instagram, Amazon, Venmo). She notices when something looks dated or clunky.
- **Her habit:** She researches on her phone in bed at 10:30pm after the kids are asleep. She'll spend 20-30 minutes comparing options.

### What She Wants from The Dental Angel
1. **"Is Invisalign actually better, or is my orthodontist upselling me?"** — She wants honest education about the difference
2. **"Is $5,200 a fair price in Houston?"** — She wants to know she's not being overcharged
3. **"What questions should I ask about the payment plan?"** — She wants to negotiate from a position of knowledge
4. **"Am I a bad parent for choosing the cheaper option?"** — She wants reassurance (indirectly)

### What Would Make Her Leave the App
- If it looks like it was designed in 2015
- If it only talks about "you" and doesn't address parents making decisions for children
- Slow loading times
- Having to re-enter information she already provided
- If the free version is so limited it feels like a bait-and-switch

### What Would Make Her Tell Her Friends About It
- Sharing a "questions to ask your orthodontist" list in her mom's group chat
- Saying "This app told me the exact price range for braces in Houston — my quote was right in the middle"
- Feeling like the app removed her guilt and gave her permission to choose what's right for her family

### UI Design Rules for Maria

| Principle | Specific Guideline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Speed** | Every screen must load instantly. She has zero patience for spinners. |
| **Polish** | The app needs to look modern and professional. No clunky layouts or outdated icons. |
| **Shareable** | Make it dead simple to share insights, question lists, or cost comparisons via text message. Big "Share" buttons where they make sense. |
| **Android parity** | Everything must work and look great on Android. Test on Galaxy devices, not just iPhone. |
| **Scannable** | She reads in F-patterns — skim headings, read first lines, skip the rest. Use bold headings, bullet points, and short paragraphs. |
| **Family context** | Chat and treatment screens should handle "my child needs..." or "my parent needs..." — not just "I need..." |
| **Value first** | Show her something useful before asking for money. The free version should be genuinely helpful, not a teaser. |

## Persona 3: Robert — "The Skeptical Researcher"

### Who She Is
- **Age:** 67
- **Location:** Phoenix, AZ
- **Occupation:** Retired mechanical engineer
- **Family:** Married 40 years. Wife Patricia manages their health appointments.
- **Phone:** iPhone 15 Pro (he researches everything before buying, including phones)

### What Happened
Robert's dentist says he needs two implants to replace teeth he lost years ago. The quote is $9,400 for two implants with crowns. He's been living without those teeth for 3 years and it's been fine. He's not sure he even needs implants. His wife says he should do it. He wants DATA before he decides.

### His Emotional State
- **Anxiety level: Low.** He's not afraid of dental work. He's done root canals and extractions without breaking a sweat.
- **Skepticism level: Very High.** He was an engineer. He wants evidence. He'll ask "why?" five times before accepting an answer.
- **Financial concern: Medium.** He can afford it, but he's frugal by nature. He wants to know: "Is this worth $9,400 to me, given I've managed fine without those teeth?"
- **Trust level: Low.** He doesn't trust apps, AI, or "Dr. Google." He's only trying this because Patricia sent him the link.
- **Decision style: Analytical.** He wants to compare options, see data, understand trade-offs. He'll spend an hour reading before making a move.

### How He Uses His Phone
- **Comfort level: Medium.** He's technical (he was an engineer), but he doesn't like apps. He prefers websites. He'll use an app if it's useful, but he judges it harshly.
- **What trips him up:**
  - Small text (he won't admit it, but his eyesight isn't what it was)
  - "Cutesy" design that feels unserious — he wants substance, not personality
  - Being forced to chat when he'd rather read an article
  - Upsells and marketing language — instant credibility killer
  - Vague answers — if the app says "it depends," he's gone
- **His reading style:** He reads everything. He scrolls to the bottom of pages. He reads disclaimers. He'll notice if something is inconsistent.
- **His typing:** He types full sentences with proper punctuation. He'll ask detailed, specific questions.

### What He Wants from The Dental Angel
1. **"What are the actual risks of NOT getting implants?"** — He wants medical facts, not scare tactics
2. **"What are ALL my options?"** — Implants vs. bridge vs. partial denture vs. doing nothing. Pros and cons of each.
3. **"What's the real cost range in Phoenix?"** — He'll verify this independently
4. **"What are the success rates and failure rates?"** — He wants percentages and data, not reassurance

### What Would Make Him Leave the App
- If the AI gives a wishy-washy answer: "Every situation is different..." — he wants specifics
- If it feels like an ad for dental work — he'll assume it's funded by dentists
- If it doesn't cite any sources or basis for its claims
- If the "Dr. Angel" persona feels fake or gimmicky to him
- If the app pushes him to purchase within the first 2 minutes

### What Would Make Him Recommend It
- If the AI gives him a genuinely useful comparison of options he hadn't considered
- If the cost data matches what he finds independently
- If it respects his intelligence — gives real information, not oversimplified fluff
- He'd tell Patricia. Patricia would tell their entire social circle.

### UI Design Rules for Robert

| Principle | Specific Guideline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Substance over style** | He doesn't care about animations or pretty illustrations. He wants clear information, well-organized. |
| **Reading mode** | Some users (like Robert) want to READ, not chat. Consider having treatment info available as articles/pages, not just through the AI chat. |
| **Credibility signals** | Show where information comes from. "Based on ADA survey data" or "Common range per FAIR Health" — not just numbers with no source. |
| **Decision tools** | He loves comparison tables. "Option A vs. Option B vs. Option C" with pros, cons, and costs side by side. The Decision Trees feature is perfect for him — make it prominent. |
| **No hard sell** | The upgrade prompts should be subtle for users like Robert. If he feels sold to, he'll write a 1-star review with 500 words explaining why. |
| **Depth available** | Let him dig deeper. If the AI mentions "bone loss risk," let him tap to learn more. Layers of information, not walls of text all at once. |
| **Professional tone option** | The warm, friendly Dr. Angel tone is great for Susan. Robert might prefer a more straightforward, clinical tone. Consider an option (in settings) for "Conversational" vs. "Detailed" response style. |

## Persona 4: Priya — "The Long-Distance Daughter"

### Who She Is
- **Age:** 35
- **Location:** Chicago, IL (parents in Mumbai, India)
- **Occupation:** Software project manager
- **Family:** Married, no kids. Parents are in India, ages 65 and 68.
- **Phone:** iPhone 15, tech-savvy, uses dozens of apps

### What Happened
Priya's father called from India. His dentist recommended removing 4 teeth and getting a partial denture. Priya is alarmed — that sounds aggressive. She can't be there in person. She wants to help her dad understand whether this is the right call, or whether he should get another opinion locally. The treatment would cost about 80,000 INR ($950 USD), which is significant in India.

### Her Emotional State
- **Anxiety level: High.** Not about dental work — about being far away and unable to help her dad in person.
- **Guilt: High.** She feels like she should be there. She's trying to make up for distance by being extra helpful remotely.
- **Control: Low.** She can't go to the appointment. She can't see the X-rays. She's relying on her dad's description, which is vague.
- **Trust level: Medium.** She trusts Indian dentists in general, but her parents tend to just accept whatever a doctor says without questioning.
- **Urgency: Medium.** Her dad's appointment for the extractions is in 10 days.

### How She Uses Her Phone
- **Comfort level: Expert.** She's a project manager at a tech company. She evaluates apps for a living.
- **Her expectations:** Seamless, modern, fast. She'll judge the app in 30 seconds.
- **Her use case:** She wants to learn enough from the app to guide a phone call with her dad. She might even share the app with him (he uses WhatsApp and not much else).

### What She Wants from The Dental Angel
1. **"Is extracting 4 teeth normal, or is that aggressive?"** — She wants a sanity check
2. **"What alternatives should my dad ask about?"** — She wants a list she can WhatsApp him
3. **"What questions should he ask before agreeing?"** — A checklist he can bring to the appointment
4. **"Does this app work for patients in India?"** — She needs international guidance

### What Would Make Her Leave the App
- If it's US-only with no international relevance
- If she can't easily share content with her dad via WhatsApp or text
- If the app doesn't handle "I'm asking for someone else" well
- If the onboarding is slow — she wants to get answers NOW, not set up a profile

### What Would Make Her Recommend It
- If she can send her dad a clear, translated-to-simple-English explanation
- If the "questions to ask" feature lets her customize and share a list
- If the app explicitly says "You can use this to help a family member"

### UI Design Rules for Priya

| Principle | Specific Guideline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **"Asking for someone else" flow** | The chat should gracefully handle "My father was told he needs..." — not assume the user IS the patient. |
| **Sharing is critical** | WhatsApp-friendly sharing. When she shares a question list or treatment explanation, it should look good in a WhatsApp message, not just be a link. |
| **International support** | Don't show US-specific pricing for international queries. Show universal guidance: "Get a comparison quote," "Ask what alternatives exist," "Ask for the X-ray to share with another dentist." |
| **Skip the fluff** | She doesn't need a warm welcome screen. She needs answers in 60 seconds. Consider a "Quick Start" option that skips onboarding. |
| **Cross-device thinking** | She might start on her phone and want to share something her dad can read on his phone. Content should be self-contained when shared — no "open the app to see more." |

## Persona 5: James — "The Uninsured Worker"

### Who She Is
- **Age:** 34
- **Location:** Rural Tennessee
- **Occupation:** HVAC technician
- **Family:** Wife and two young kids (4 and 7)
- **Phone:** Older Samsung Galaxy A13, cracked screen, limited storage

### What Happened
James has been avoiding the dentist for 5 years because he doesn't have dental insurance. He finally went because a tooth was killing him. The dentist said he needs a root canal ($1,100), a crown ($1,200), two fillings ($400), and a deep cleaning ($800). Total: $3,500. He makes $22/hour. He doesn't have $3,500. He's panicking.

### His Emotional State
- **Anxiety level: Extreme.** He's in pain, he's embarrassed about his teeth, and now he's looking at a bill that's two months of rent.
- **Shame: High.** He knows he should have gone sooner. He doesn't want to be judged.
- **Financial stress: Extreme.** This is a crisis. He's wondering which bills he can skip to pay for this.
- **Trust level: Low.** He doesn't trust that ALL of this work is necessary. He wonders: "Are they trying to get me for everything since I finally came in?"
- **Urgency: High.** The tooth pain is real. The root canal can't wait.

### How He Uses His Phone
- **Comfort level: Medium.** He watches YouTube, uses Facebook Marketplace, and texts. He's not downloading a lot of apps.
- **His phone limitations:** Older phone, limited storage, sometimes slow. The app needs to be lightweight.
- **When he uses it:** During lunch break (30 minutes), or in the truck between jobs. He doesn't have hours to research.
- **His typing:** Short messages. He's not writing paragraphs. He might type: "do i really need all this done at once or can i just fix the one that hurts"

### What He Wants from The Dental Angel
1. **"Do I really need ALL of this right now?"** — He wants to know what's urgent vs. what can wait
2. **"What's the bare minimum I need to do?"** — He needs to triage by urgency AND cost
3. **"Is there a cheaper way to fix the one that hurts?"** — He wants to know his options
4. **"How do I not get ripped off?"** — He wants to know if these prices are fair for rural Tennessee

### What Would Make Him Leave the App
- If it assumes everyone has insurance and money
- If it talks to him like a doctor talks to a patient (above him)
- If the app is slow or takes up too much storage
- If the free version doesn't actually help with his specific question
- If it makes him feel judged for waiting 5 years

### What Would Make Him Tell Someone About It
- If it tells him "The root canal is urgent, but the fillings can wait 3-6 months, and the deep cleaning can wait too — so you're looking at $2,300 now, not $3,500"
- If the prices it shows match his area (rural, lower cost of living)
- If Dr. Angel's tone makes him feel like a normal person, not a patient who messed up

### UI Design Rules for James

| Principle | Specific Guideline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Lightweight** | The app must work fast on older phones. Minimize animations, large images, and heavy assets. |
| **Instant value** | He's not going through an onboarding flow. He needs to ask his question in under 30 seconds. |
| **No shame** | The AI should never say "You should have come in sooner" or anything that implies blame. Tone: "You're here now, let's figure this out." |
| **Triage focus** | The ability to separate "urgent" from "can wait" is the single most valuable feature for this user. Make it prominent. |
| **Cost-first thinking** | Lead with costs. He doesn't care about the clinical details — he wants to know what it costs and whether it's fair. |
| **Short answers** | He's reading on a cracked screen on his lunch break. No long paragraphs. Bullet points. Bold the key numbers. |
| **Payment info** | Consider mentioning dental schools, community health centers, and payment plans. These are lifelines for uninsured patients. |

# How to Use These Personas

## When Making Any UI Decision, Ask:

1. **Can Susan use this?** (Biggest text, simplest flow, most obvious buttons)
2. **Does Maria think it looks modern?** (Polish, speed, shareability)
3. **Does Robert trust this?** (Credibility, sources, substance)
4. **Can Priya share this with her dad?** (International, sharable, works for "someone else")
5. **Does James feel welcomed?** (No shame, cost-first, fast, lightweight)

## Priority Order for Design Decisions

**Susan is your primary persona.** She represents the largest user segment (women 45-65 researching their own treatment). If a design choice works great for Maria but confuses Susan, go with Susan.

**But never alienate Robert or James.** Robert is your word-of-mouth engine (his wife will tell everyone). James is your most grateful user (and most likely to leave a 5-star review if you help him).

## Top UI Improvements Suggested by These Personas

| # | Improvement | Which Personas | Why |
|---|------------|----------------|-----|
| 1 | **Raise minimum font to 16px** (18px for chat & treatment content) | Susan, Robert | Research shows 32% better comprehension for 45+ adults |
| 2 | **Add "skip" option on welcome card** | Priya, James | Not everyone wants to set up a profile before getting help |
| 3 | **Add "Asking for someone else" option in chat** | Priya, Maria | Parents and adult children are a huge use case |
| 4 | **Show urgency triage** ("Needs attention now" vs. "Can wait") | James, Susan | The #1 thing anxious patients want to know |
| 5 | **Add source citations to cost data** | Robert | "Based on FAIR Health data for your area" builds trust |
| 6 | **Make share buttons more prominent** | Maria, Priya | Sharing is how this app grows — word of mouth |
| 7 | **Reduce spacing between upgrade banner and first message** | Susan, James | The upgrade banner shouldn't push content down on small screens |
| 8 | **Add "What's urgent?" as a quick-start prompt** | James, Susan | Most common first question — make it one tap |
| 9 | **Decision Trees should be discoverable from chat** | Robert | When AI mentions "alternatives," link directly to the comparison |
| 10 | **WhatsApp-friendly share format** | Priya | International users share via WhatsApp, not text message |

## The Emotional Journey (All Personas Share This)

```
STAGE 1: Shock (0-30 minutes after appointment)
"Wait, I need WHAT? And it costs HOW MUCH?"
   -> They're not ready for an app yet. They're processing.

STAGE 2: Confusion + Doubt (1-24 hours later)  <<<< THIS IS WHEN THEY FIND THE APP
"Is this really necessary? Is the price fair? What are my options?"
   -> They Google something. They find The Dental Angel.
   -> THE APP MUST DELIVER VALUE IN 60 SECONDS.
   -> This is the moment that matters. If Susan can't figure out what to do
      in the first screen she sees, she closes the app and never comes back.

STAGE 3: Research (1-7 days)
"Let me compare options and prices."
   -> They're now in the app. They use the chat, check costs, look at decision trees.
   -> This is where the paid tiers make sense. They see the value.

STAGE 4: Decision (3-14 days)
"I know what to ask. I know my options. I'm ready to decide."
   -> They use the "Questions to Ask" feature.
   -> This is the moment they recommend the app to a friend.

STAGE 5: Resolution (After the appointment)
"I went back and asked those questions. I feel good about my decision."
   -> This is when 5-star reviews happen.
```

**The entire app should be optimized for Stage 2.** That first 60 seconds. A confused, anxious person who just wants ONE clear answer.
